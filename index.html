"Good Luck With That"

Understanding the sarcasm behind the words.

by Daby

Literal vs. Real Meaning

While "good luck with that" literally means wishing someone success, it's very often used sarcastically to express disbelief or doubt. It's what you say when someone proposes something difficult, unrealistic, or even foolish. It's the English version of a passive-aggressive "I don't think so."

The real meaning depends heavily on tone and context. Let's see how it's used in real situations.

How Would a Brazilian Say This?

In Brazil, we don't have a single phrase that perfectly matches "good luck with that," but we have a few that capture the same sarcastic tone depending on the situation.

  • "Boa sorte, viu!": The literal translation, but said with a skeptical, drawn-out "viu!" at the end. The tone is everything.
  • "√â pra rir ou pra chorar?": A rhetorical question used to express disbelief at how difficult or ridiculous a situation is.
  • "Eu duvido.": A more direct and less common phrase, but it gets the point across.
